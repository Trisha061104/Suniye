    Cochlear Implant

    A cochlear implant is a small, electronic device that can help people with severe hearing loss or deafness hear sounds.

    How it works

    The implant has an external portion that sits behind the ear and another portion surgically placed under the skin. The device bypasses damaged parts of the ear and directly stimulates the auditory nerve. Electrical signals generated by the implant are sent to the brain, which interprets these signals as sounds.

    Benefits

    Cochlear implants can help people understand speech and perceive sounds, even if they couldn’t with hearing aids. They can improve communication, social interaction, and speech production skills, especially when combined with lipreading.

    Surgery

    Cochlear implants are placed through a surgical procedure. The success of the implant depends on the individual’s condition and the rehabilitation process. Speech therapy is often essential to maximize benefits.

    Risks

    The surgery is relatively safe, but there are some risks, such as infection, device malfunction, or facial nerve injury. These should be discussed with your doctor before proceeding.

    Children

    Children with severe hearing loss may be candidates for a cochlear implant as young as 9 months old. The earlier the implant is placed, the better the chance the child will benefit from it.
